# Blog Post Ideas

A running list of post ideas. Add new ones with the `add-idea` skill, or just append below.

<!-- Format: - [ ] One-line idea — optional note on the angle/why -->

- [ ] What is "real work," actually? — Cold-open with a "two observers" thought experiment (one sees furious typing for eight hours; one sees a person who wrote three sentences + a diagram that aligned five engineers — who did more?) to expose the dichotomy to a reader blind to one side. Then argue the historical line: steering was ALWAYS the senior skill; the best architects always produced less visible output — AI didn't create the gap between "looks like work" and "is valuable," it just made it impossible to ignore. Evidence: the design doc, the Gemini refactor. Core feeling: "I did my best work and it looked like I did nothing." Cross-references the TL identity-rupture post.
- [ ] The design doc as the real deliverable — Lightweight case study, publish AFTER "What is real work." Under a brutal deadline (four weeks, one lost to vacation, a Google I/O launch), the doc was the bottleneck, not the code. Used agentic AI (duckie) to produce a polished, diagram-heavy design doc in two days; alignment across five engineers is what actually shipped the project. Reframe line: "I used AI to write the document, not the code — and that's why we shipped." Proof-in-miniature under the "real work" thesis.
- [ ] The identity rupture that predated AI — Standalone memoir; cross-references "What is real work." Becoming a TL meant writing less code and not recognizing my own work as work — a crisis that existed BEFORE LLMs were capable; the AI era just resolved a tension already there. Arc: leadership stripped the keystroke-based sense of worth, I sat in the discomfort, tooling eventually caught up. Then generalize: the identity vertigo a TL felt about delegation is what junior engineers feel now about AI — AI democratized the crisis. Takeaway/CTA: junior engineers should be braver and let go of the click-clack comfort zone sooner than I did.
- [ ] "Throw it at the AI first" — Practical/evangelism piece. The habit I push on my engineers: attempt the full problem with the latest AI tool before manually cutting it down. Depth is the hidden prerequisite — "AI first" only works if you can catch when the AI is subtly wrong (plausible but bad). The tells: define the goal with all its nuance so you have exit criteria and a quality measure; don't be afraid to make the AI provide evidence for its solutions. Tension: I tell juniors to lead with AI while I earned the judgment to catch its mistakes — name that unstated dependency. Shares a backbone (define-the-goal + demand-evidence rigor) with the "Lego to concrete" post; keep separate.
- [ ] Agent skills are the new snake oil — Punchy, opinionated piece. The snake-oil wagon never sold a chemical, it sold a story: a testimonial, a confident posture, a crowd nodding along. Swap the wagon for a GitHub repo and the tonic for a `SKILL.md` and the script is identical — "this skill made my agent 10x better," with no held-out eval, no baseline, no reported failure modes, no way to attribute the win to the skill vs. the model improving underneath it. The actual product is clout: authoring is cheap, measuring is expensive, claiming impact is free and pays in followers — and sincerity isn't evidence (the salesman often believed his own pitch). Land the bar that almost nobody clears: a runnable eval, a baseline delta, the failure cases, versioned against a model+date. Close on how snake oil ended not because buyers got smarter but because measurement and labeling made claims checkable. Possible self-aware footnote: this very repo runs on Claude Code skills.
- [ ] From stacking Lego to pouring concrete and chiseling — Flagship thesis post. Old model of building software was Lego: precise, incremental, one block at a time. New physics: pour an approximate slab cheaply, measure the gap to the goal, then chisel down — because both pouring AND chiseling just got cheap with AI. Not a productivity tip; a different construction philosophy. Lead with the metaphor COLD (Lego vs. concrete) before any AI talk, let the image land, then bring evidence + the rigor twist. Evidence: the Gemini CLI refactor that executed a fast sweeping abstraction from a single high-level comment (pour-and-chisel in miniature). Hidden prerequisite (shared with "Throw it at the AI first"): you can only chisel well if you've defined the goal sharply enough to measure the gap, and if you make the AI show evidence. Keep separate.
